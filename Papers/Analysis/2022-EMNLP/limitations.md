# Limitations

## Technical Limitations
- **Faithfulness of explanations not evaluated**: The study does not explicitly measure whether model-generated Natural Language Explanations (NLEs) are faithful to the model's actual reasoning process. The authors acknowledge this and suggest using methods from contemporaneous literature (Sia et al., 2022; Chan et al., 2022) for future evaluation.
- **Negative rationale quality scores**: Predicted rationales from both T5e-SNLI and T5FLUTE yield negative Rationale Quality scores, indicating that model-generated explanations are imperfect and can introduce noise that hurts prediction. Only gold rationales achieve positive RQ scores.
- **Limited model architecture exploration**: Only T5 (3B) is used as the base model. The paper does not explore whether other architectures or larger models would yield different results.
- **Joint model limitations**: While joint self-rationalizing models are preferred over pipeline models, the generated rationales are still not reliably useful for downstream prediction.

## Dataset Limitations
- **Narrow coverage of figurative language types**: FLUTE covers only four types (sarcasm, simile, metaphor, idiom). Other forms such as hyperbole, irony, personification, and litotes are not included.
- **Sarcasm diversity**: The sarcasm portion captures only the most common type of incongruity (between sarcastic context and sentiment). Other forms — situational, underplayed, or dramatic sarcasm — are not represented, and their explanations would differ.
- **Cultural and contextual scope**: Figurative language draws on a wide variety of cultural knowledge and contexts. The dataset, while diverse, is still a first step and may not capture this breadth.
- **GPT-3 dependency**: The dataset relies on GPT-3 for generation, which may introduce systematic biases or limitations inherent to that model's training data and capabilities.
- **Size**: With 9,000 instances, FLUTE is significantly smaller than general NLI datasets like e-SNLI (366K+ training examples), which may limit the training of large models from scratch.

## Experimental Limitations
- **Single baseline comparison**: The primary comparison is against e-SNLI. Comparisons with other explanation datasets (e.g., CoS-E) or other figurative language datasets are not provided.
- **Limited human evaluation sample**: Human evaluation is conducted on only 200 samples (50 per type), which may not be representative of the full dataset.
- **Moderate inter-annotator agreement**: Krippendorff's α of 0.45 indicates only moderate agreement among human evaluators, suggesting subjective variability in explanation quality assessment.
- **No cross-dataset generalization experiments**: The paper does not evaluate whether training on FLUTE improves performance on other figurative language tasks or datasets.

## Threats to Validity
- **GPT-3 bias**: While the authors verified that FLUTE does not contain toxic text, GPT-3 has been shown to exhibit societal biases (Sheng et al., 2021), religious stereotypes (Abid et al., 2021), and gender stereotypes (Borchers et al., 2022). Subtle biases may exist in the generated content despite manual inspection.
- **Expert verification as a bottleneck**: The quality of the dataset depends heavily on expert verification, which may not scale and introduces subjectivity.
- **Crowd worker quality for sarcasm**: Despite qualification tests, crowd workers' ability to generate high-quality sarcastic edits may vary, potentially introducing noise.
- **Domain specificity**: Results may not generalize beyond the specific figurative language types and domains represented in FLUTE.
- **Faithfulness gap**: There is no guarantee that explanations generated by models trained on FLUTE reflect true reasoning rather than plausible-sounding post-hoc rationalizations.
